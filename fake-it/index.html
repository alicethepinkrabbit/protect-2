Medium / Format: Generative JavaScript algorithm, minted as NFTs (Art Blocks); printed book; physical “merch” incarnations; installations. It consists of 700 unique NFTs.Description: The system scrapes or aggregates text-graphic tropes from social media (especially inspirational / wellness graphic memes) and remixes them via algorithm into pastel-gradient images with overlaid phrases. Some outputs are recognizably derivative of motivational rhetoric (“You are enough,” “Believe in yourself”) while others subvert or rewrite them ironically (e.g. “REPEAT, OVERTHINK, OBSESS”). The project exists across digital, object, and spatial modalities: you can generate new images, browse past editions in the book, or encounter physical artifacts in a gift shop scenario. Conceptual Basis: The project interrogates how social media languages (affirmations, self-help graphics, “girl boss” tropes) reify norms of positivity, productivity, and self-optimization. By algorithmically remixing these tropes, Maya Man questions sincerity vs. simulacrum, co-option vs. resistance. The work speaks to how motivational language becomes commodified and flattening, while raising questions about agency within generative systems. Exhibitions / Versions: The original NFT drop on Art Blocks during Miami Art Week 2022. The FAKE IT TILL YOU MAKE IT book was published by Heavy Manners Library in 2024, collecting ~700 editions, essays, code, etc. A “Gift Shop” installation (Feb–Mar 2024, Los Angeles) turned outputs into physical merch (stickers, apparel, prints) mixing commerce and critique. bitforms gallery+1; Related solo and group exhibitions around code art, wellness critique, and digital aesthetics. Cultural / Artistic Impact: FAKE IT TILL YOU MAKE IT has catalyzed discourse on the limits of irony in digital critique. In The Brooklyn Rail, Kat Kitay argues that while the project critiques “digital girlhood,” it sometimes reproduces the very clichés it lampoons. Some view it as ironic, others see it as complicity. The project sits in lineage with artists who adopt “digital girl” aesthetics (Molly Soda, Petra Cortright, Ann Hirsch). It also participates in the generative / algorithmic art movement, where aesthetics are co-produced with code, not simply represented. Its hybrid form (digital → book → merch) demonstrates how net art is evolving to cross media boundaries.
