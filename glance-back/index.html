Maya Man’s project Glance Back takes the computer screen—from which so much of our identity now emerges—as both site and subject, asking what it means when your device turns the gaze back on you. The tool is distributed as a browser extension; at a random moment once per day, when the user opens a new tab, the extension snaps a candid photograph of the user, prompts them with the question “What are you thinking about?”, and then stores both the image and the text response in an archive on the user’s local machine.

What stands out in Glance Back is its interrogation of the polished self-image that dominates social media. Instead of asking the user to post another perfect selfie or stage a curated moment, the extension captures a moment when the user is — whether aware of it or not — encountering their screen. Maya Man describes it as an antidote to stereotyped “posed” online imagery: the photograph need not be flattering, the user need not share it publicly; it is just a record between them and their machine.

Because the archive is stored locally and not expected to be shared, the project shifts the relationship between user and device. The computer, often seen as a passive tool for consumption or broadcasting, becomes an interlocutor: it silently watches and prompts a response. The architecture of the extension also evokes the algorithmic gaze that defines so much of our online existence. Glance Back flips the gaze back, exposing how we are being seen while we consume or produce content.

n terms of influence and significance, Glance Back resonates for a number of reasons. First, it engages with themes of surveillance, self-image and the behind-the-scenes of digital identity: by forcing a candid moment, the project highlights how “authenticity” itself is often mediated by technology and expectation. Second, the work underscores the idea that the boundary between performance and everyday life is thinning when so much of our time is spent interacting with screens. Third, its format—a simple Chrome extension—demonstrates how accessible tools can become platforms for critical reflection. The project has gathered thousands of users, creating a distributed archive of screened selves that, while private, constitute a collective practice of reflection.

Finally, Glance Back matters because it invites users to pause: to look at the moment when the screen opens, when a new tab appears, when the machine asks you “what are you thinking about?” It asks whether you’re simply consuming, or whether you’re aware of how you’re being observed—or observing yourself. In a culture of endless sharing and curated identity, Glance Back offers a slow, quiet interruption—a moment for you and your screen to meet.
